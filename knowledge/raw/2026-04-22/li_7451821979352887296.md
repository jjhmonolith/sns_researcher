## 원문

The company that automates the fastest will not win.
The company that automates the right things first will.
Speed without direction is just expensive chaos.
I have seen it happen more than once.
Here is how the most effective IT leaders I know are thinking about prioritising their automation roadmap:
🎯 THEY RANK BY IMPACT AND REPEATABILITY
High volume. Low variation. High cost of human error.
That is your first automation candidate.
Not the flashy use case you can present to the board.
The boring, repetitive workflow that is quietly draining two hours from every team member every single day.
🎯 THEY CALCULATE THE COST OF DOING NOTHING
Most roadmap conversations focus on the cost of investment.
The better question is: what is this inefficiency costing us every month we delay?
When you put a number on inaction, priorities shift very quickly.
🎯 THEY SEQUENCE FOR QUICK WINS BEFORE TRANSFORMATION
A 90-day visible win builds more internal support for your three-year roadmap than any business case document ever will.
Politics inside organisations are real.
Proof beats persuasion every time.
🎯 THEY BUILD FOR INTEGRATION, NOT ISOLATION
The worst automation projects create new silos.
A bot that works brilliantly but cannot talk to your CRM, your ERP, or your reporting stack creates a different kind of manual work.
Always design for connection from day one.
Here is what nobody tells you about automation roadmaps:
The technical decisions are the easiest ones you will make.
The hard decisions are about priorities, politics, and people.
Who loses influence when a process gets automated?
Who needs to champion this for it to actually get used?
Which department needs a win badly enough to become your internal advocate?
Answer those questions first.
Then build your roadmap.
What is one automation your team keeps talking about
but has never actually started?

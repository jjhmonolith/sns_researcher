## 원문

Otters.
The accidental benchmark of AI visual thinking.
Back in 2023, Wharton professor Ethan Mollick was on a plane with his teenage daughter when the wifi cut out. Otters were her favourite animal. So he typed "otter on a plane using wifi" into a new AI image generator just as the connection came back.
The tweet went viral. That prompt has been his go-to test of AI image progress ever since.
Melted fur in early 2022, weird fingers and a wonky keyboard by the end of that year. Photorealism by 2025. It's the image generation equivalent of the "Will Smith eating spaghetti" test in video.
Yesterday he ran it on OpenAI's new ChatGPT Images 2.0 and got back a significantly better result: Scientist otter standing next to a full slide deck with clean charts, crisp column headers and actually legible text.
A pun in the margin reading "Further research is otter-ly necessary."
Images have moved from decoration to readable slides, reliable infographics, academic-looking papers and dad jokes, all from a single prompt.
💡The technical unlock is "reasoning" before generation:
ChatGPT Images 2.0 pulls OpenAI's O-series reasoning into the image pipeline. The model breaks a prompt into parts, decides how they fit together, draws on uploaded files and external context, then generates.
It holds character and object consistency across up to 8 images from a single prompt. From months of playing with multimodal models in production, this is one of the hardest problems to solve at scale. Text renders legibly inside dense compositions: diagrams, menus, posters, multilingual layouts. Up to 2K in product, 4K via API beta, multiple aspect ratios.
Google's Nano Banana Pro has been running this for months and remains a personal favourite. Similar architectural direction: think, plan, then draw. Too early to call which performs better at scale, but my tests this morning over coffee look very impressive in quality, speed and consistency.
Also an interesting evolution: the "diffusion vs autoregressive" argument matters less every month. Configuration discipline seems to matter more (as with agentic frameworks).
How the prompt is fed into the pipeline, what context you feed in, how you chain calls for multi-panel outputs. That's where output quality actually lives now. In production, it's about having enough observability and QA checks to catch drift, which at scale, *will* happen.
Because when image outputs carry factual content, observability stops being optional.
And at enterprise grade, the safety bar keeps rising as these models get better. Filtering now runs at the perception layer rather than just the prompt, policy enforcement happens in real time, and image traceability and safety is based on standards like SynthID and C2PA.
The benchmark used to be whether the image looked real. Now it's whether the content inside holds up to fact-checking scrutiny and production-readiness.

## 원문

Most organizations think their AI governance problem is about control.
Permissions.
Policies.
Monitoring.
Frameworks.
But that’s not the whole story.
Things often break earlier.
At the moment a decision is made.
An AI system produces an output.
It *looks* right.
It *seems* valid.
It *fits* the process.
And someone acts on it.
That moment is rarely governed with the same rigor as the AI system itself.
It's not about missing controls.
It's about missing *decision design*.
Specifically: ownership, criteria, and boundaries.
The result?
• Decisions appear legitimate, even when they're not
• Ownership blurs the moment outcomes are questioned
• Teams interpret, rather than follow shared logic
• Speed amplifies ambiguity, instead of clarity
We've approached governance as:
→ policy enforcement
→ risk mitigation
→ architectural constraint
All necessary pieces.
But incomplete on their own.
The shift?
Governance isn't just about controlling AI systems.
It's about designing AI-informed decisions.
That means:
• Defining decision ownership *before* outputs are generated
• Making decision criteria explicit and testable—not assumed
• Embedding escalation logic into workflows (what happens if…)
• Ensuring decisions are traceable *and* attributable to a clear owner
Because as AI compresses decision cycles…
Design, not delay, becomes the critical constraint.
The organizations that scale AI effectively won't just have the best models.
They'll be the ones where decisions are:
Clear.
Structured.
Non-ambiguous.
By design.
Think of it this way: a well-designed decision process actually *creates* momentum.
Where are you seeing this play out?
Are decisions in your organization explicitly designed for AI—or still emerging ad hoc?

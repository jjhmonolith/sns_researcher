## 원문

Most AI failures don’t look like failures.
They look like normal decisions.
The system runs.
The output makes sense.
The workflow continues.
Nothing breaks.
Until later.
A wrong escalation.
A missed exception.
A decision that should have been questioned—but wasn’t.
Not because the model failed.
Because the decision system didn’t know when to doubt itself.
We’ve been focused on:
→ model accuracy
→ cost efficiency
→ tool integration
But very little on something more fundamental:
When should a system NOT decide?
That’s where most organizations are exposed.
Because without that layer:
• Every output is treated as equally valid
• Edge cases get forced into standard flows
• Teams override logic silently—without traceability
• Errors don’t stop the system… they compound through it
What’s missing isn’t more intelligence.
It’s decision boundaries.
Clear conditions for:
• when a decision is valid
• when it must be escalated
• when it should be blocked entirely
In traditional systems, we designed for execution.
In AI systems, we have to design for judgment.
And judgment starts with knowing when not to proceed.
The real question isn’t:
“How do we make better AI decisions?”
It’s:
“Where should AI stop—and hand control back?”
Because in high-speed systems…
Unbounded decisions don’t fail loudly.
They fail quietly—at scale.
How are you defining that boundary today?
Or is every output still treated as a green light?

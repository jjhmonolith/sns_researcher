## 원문

Most AI failures don’t start with large errors.
They start with decisions that seem insignificant.
"Nothing looks critical. So the system proceeds."
Each step follows logic.
Each action appears valid.
Until those small decisions accumulate.
That’s when the impact shows up.
Not because the system failed—
but because no one defined when it should stop.
We often focus on improving:
→ detection
→ accuracy
→ response speed
But far less on something more fundamental:
When does a system need to pause?
Because without that:
• minor deviations get normalized
• thresholds become flexible over time
• escalation happens too late—or not at all
• errors compound quietly across scale
The issue isn’t visibility.
It’s boundaries.
Clear conditions for:
• when a signal matters
• when it must be escalated
• when execution should not continue
In AI systems, decisions don’t fail loudly.
They drift through repetition.
And by the time the impact is visible…
The logic that allowed it is already embedded.
That’s the risk.
Not incorrect decisions.
But unquestioned ones at scale.
Where does your system pause today?
Or does everything still move forward by default?

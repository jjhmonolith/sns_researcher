Who fixes the code written by tools like Cursor, Copilot, Devin, or Claude Code?
That’s the question Animesh Koratana has been thinking about, a 22-year-old Stanford researcher.
Watching some of the earliest code-generating AI systems, he had a simple observation:
the faster AI writes code, the more important debugging becomes.
That idea eventually led him to start PlayerZero, a company focused on 𝐟𝐢𝐱𝐢𝐧𝐠 𝐜𝐨𝐝𝐞 𝐢𝐧 𝐩𝐫𝐨𝐝𝐮𝐜𝐭𝐢𝐨𝐧, which has raised $20M.
Instead of focusing on writing new code, PlayerZero looks at what’s already running in production - things like past incidents, logs, edge cases, and how systems have actually behaved over time.
The idea is to build a kind of “memory” of the system, and use that to surface what might break before it actually does.
So rather than waiting for something to fail and then digging through logs, it tries to point you to likely issues ahead of time, and in some cases, even which users might be affected.
Some early results are quite interesting:
– Zuora saw ~80% fewer support escalations and 90% less investigation time
– Cayuse reported catching ~90% of bugs before users noticed
– Around $2M in documented ROI across enterprise deployments

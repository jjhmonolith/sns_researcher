## 원문

Psychology researchers have started giving the fear of being replaced by AI a name. Four in ten workers now live with it. The fear has almost nothing to do with income.
They are calling it AI Replacement Dysfunction — not a formal diagnosis, but a conceptual framework for the anxiety millions of professionals are quietly carrying. KPMG data shows the share of workers citing AI-driven job loss as a primary fear has nearly doubled in a single year.
When I hear this, I do not hear people worrying about money. I hear people worrying about who they will be without the role.
Last month one of our consulting clients — a senior analyst at a mid-sized firm — told me he had not slept properly in weeks. His team had just started piloting an agentic workflow that did a large part of his daily work in minutes. He said, "I know the math says I should be relieved." Then he said, "I feel like a ghost in my own life."
That is not a productivity problem. That is role engulfment.
Organisational psychologists use that term for the state in which a professional role absorbs so much of the self that almost nothing else remains to carry an identity. Robert Kegan at Harvard described the mechanism underneath it more precisely than anyone. In his subject-object theory of adult development, the things we ARE are invisible to us — we look through them. The things we HAVE can be examined, questioned, and set down. Most professionals are their role. They cannot see it because they are inside it.
When a role that large begins to dissolve, the nervous system reacts the way it reacts to acute stress. Amy Arnsten's work at Yale maps what happens next: the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex goes quiet, cortisol rises, and strategic thinking gets replaced by reactive thinking. You cannot think your way out of it while it is happening.
This is what AI is actually doing to millions of people. It is not stealing their jobs. It is dissolving the identity scaffolding they built on top of those jobs — and most of them were never taught the difference.
The professionals I see thriving are not the most technically skilled. They are the ones who moved their role from subject to object. Who they are no longer sits inside the work. It sits alongside it.
I studied clinical hypnosis in Germany specifically to understand this process — the non-conscious ways the self binds itself to external structures, and how that binding can be loosened. It is not a fringe practice. It is the operating system behind every decision you think you are making rationally.
Every leader I work with now faces the same unspoken test. Not "can you adopt AI?" — that is the easy question. The real one is: who are you if the work you are known for becomes the work a machine does in a second?
If your entire job function were automated tomorrow, which part of you would remain — and is that part anything you have ever actually invested in?

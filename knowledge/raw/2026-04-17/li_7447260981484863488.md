## 원문

AI should never make a management decision.
As a former classroom practitioner, I have always embraced technology when it has been proven to improve student outcomes. But do we risk letting AI make management decisions when it comes to designing lessons and determining the learning pathways of students?
That's why I wasn't completely shocked to hear that South Korea had downgraded its investments into AI-powered digital textbooks after investing 1.2 trillion won — approximately $850 million. This seems to be a common theme with AI adoption, and with education technology more broadly. The failure stemmed from rushed implementation without sufficient pilot testing, teacher training, or stakeholder engagement. It's a story as old as time in the education sector. Every child should have an iPad. Every classroom an interactive smartboard. (I'll never forget having to recalibrate those things every single day.)
It is well established in education research that technology adoption follows a U-shaped curve — the more technology, the better, up to a point, after which it becomes detrimental and a distraction. The OECD PISA report alludes to an "inverted-U" relationship: moderate ICT use outperforms no use, but heavy use correlates with significantly worse outcomes. The OECD is clear on this: learning depends far more on the teacher than on the use of computers. The same principle applies to AI adoption in organisations.
This is where AI Governance and Literacy training becomes critical — ensuring AI doesn't deskill people, but instead builds resilience. This is what I do. Not replacing the teacher, but enhancing them. Not another tool that distracts and exhausts, but one that genuinely supports.
I provide responsible AI training for schools looking to implement thoughtfully, without overwhelming staff or students. If your digital council or leadership team wants guidance, let's talk.
